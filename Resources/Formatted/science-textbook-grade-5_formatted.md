## CHAPTER 1  
# Structure of Living Things

### Key Vocabulary  
- **cell**: the smallest part of a living thing that can carry out processes of life  
- **cell membrane**: a thin outer layer of a cell  
- **cytoplasm**: the gel-like material inside the cell that holds all the other inner parts of the cell  
- **nucleus**: a large, round structure at the center of a cell that controls all the activities of a cell  
- **mitochondrion**: the part of a cell that breaks down food and turns it into energy for the cell  
- **vacuole**: a cell part that holds food, water, and wastes  
- **cell wall**: a hard outer layer of a plant cell that protects the cell and provides support  
- **chloroplast**: a part of a plant cell that uses energy from sunlight to make food  
- **organism**: an individual living thing that can carry out all its own life activities  
- **tissue**: a group of similar cells that do the same job in an organism  

### More Vocabulary  
- **organ**: a body part made of different kinds of tissues that work together to do a certain job  
- **organ system**: a group of organs that work together to do a certain job  
- **kingdom**: the broadest group into which living things are classified  
- **vertebrate**: an animal that has a backbone  
- **invertebrate**: an animal that does not have a backbone  
- **vascular**: any plant that has tubes for moving water and other materials to where they are needed  
- **nonvascular**: any plant that soaks up water from the ground directly into its cells  
- **fungus**: an organism that cannot make its own food, but instead absorbs food from decaying organisms  
- **bacteria**: one-celled living things that do not have a nucleus  
- **protist**: a one- or many-celled organism that can either make, eat, or absorb food

# Cells

### What are plants and animals made of?  
All living things are made of **cells (SELZ)**. A **cell** is the smallest part of a living thing that can carry out life activities such as taking in food and growing.

**Cells** are the building blocks of all living things. For example, your body, a pet dog or cat, a tree, and even a blade of grass are made of cells. Different kinds of cells have different functions: plant cells can make food and store water, while animal cells help take in food as animals do not make their own food.

Plant cells often have **boxlike shapes** that fit closely together, providing support for a plant. Animal cells have more **rounded shapes** which allow for movement.

### How can cells be seen?  
Cells are very small, so you need a **microscope (MIGH•kruh•skohp)** to see them. A microscope makes things look bigger. Cells were first seen under a microscope over **400 years ago**. Scientists took almost **200 years** to understand all living things are made of cells.

---

### Microscope Timeline  
- **1595**: Zacharias Janssen creates the first compound microscope.  
- **1665**: English scientist Robert Hooke studies slices of cork and calls the tiny boxes he sees “cells,” a Latin word meaning “little rooms.”  
- **1670s**: Dutch scientist Anton van Leeuwenhoek improves lens technology to magnify between 75 and 200 times.  
- **1860s–1890s**: Scientists develop new ways of staining cells to better see and study them.  
- **1940s**: Electron microscopes magnify **40,000 times** more than previous microscopes.  
- **1982**: Scientists build the scanning tunneling microscope that allows you to see individual blood cells.

### How do cells work together?  
Many-celled organisms have different kinds of cells like blood cells, muscle cells, and nerve cells. Cells of the same kind work together in groups called **tissues (TISH•ew)**.

Examples:  
- Animals: muscle tissue, blood, nerves, bone, skin  
- Plants: tissues that carry water from roots to stems, and flesh of fruits

  - Animals: digestive system, transport system, skeletal system

- Phyla are divided into **classes**.  
- Classes are divided into **orders**.  
- Orders are divided into **families**.  
- Families are divided into **genera** (singular: genus).

## What are Plants?

- All plants are **many-celled living things**.  
- They can all **produce their own food**.  

### Types of Plants

- Most common plants are **vascular (VAS•kyuh•luhr) plants**.  
- **Vascular plants** have tubes running up and down inside, bringing water and minerals from the ground to roots, stems, and leaves, and transporting food to other parts.  
- **Nonvascular (non•VAS•kyuh•luhr) plants** like mosses do **not** have tubes. They soak up water directly from the soil into their cells, so they must grow very close to the ground.

## What are Fungi?

- Mushrooms grow from the ground and are often mistaken for plants. However, a **mushroom is a fungus (FUNG•guhs)**.  
- A fungus **cannot make its own food**, unlike plants. Instead, it **absorbs food from dead organisms**.  
- **Fungi (FUN•ji)** can be one-celled or many-celled.  
- They can be **helpful or harmful**.

### Helpful Fungi

- Break down dead organisms, enriching soil  
- Yeasts make bread rise  
- Used in medicines like **penicillin**  

### Harmful Fungi

- Wild mushrooms can be poisonous  
- Cause diseases such as athlete’s foot  
- Attack crops like wheat rust and corn smut

## What are Bacteria?

- **Bacteria (bak•TEER•ee•uh)** are **one-celled living things**.  
- Unlike other cells, bacteria **do not have a nucleus**, but they do have a **cell membrane** and **cytoplasm**. Most have **cell walls**.  
- Bacteria belong to **two kingdoms**.  
- **True bacteria** may cause diseases, but many are helpful, such as bacteria in your digestive system which help digest food.  
- Bacteria are used to produce yogurt and other foods.

## What are Protists?

- **Protists (PRO•tists)** are living things that do not fit into any other kingdom.  
- They may be one-celled or many-celled.  
- Some protists make their own food like plants.  
- Some eat other living things like animals.  
- Some break down dead organisms like fungi.  
- Protists are much simpler than plants, animals, and fungi.

## Lesson 1: Vascular Plants

### What are vascular plants?

- Trees, bushes, grass, and plants with vegetables or fruits are **vascular plants**.  
- These plants have special tissues that form **thin tubes** inside the plant.  
- The tubes carry water and other materials up and down the plant.  

### Main parts of a vascular plant:

- **Roots**  
  - Anchor plants to the ground  
  - Take in water and minerals from the soil  
  - Store food made by the plant (in some plants)  

- **Stems**  
  - Support the plant above ground  
  - Move materials from roots to leaves and vice versa  

- **Leaves**  
  - Make food for the plant  

### Types of vascular plants:

- **Seedless plants:** like ferns, which do not have seeds but grow from spores.  
- **Seed plants:** most familiar vascular plants that grow from seeds.

---

## Classifying Vascular Plants

### Seedless Plants

- Plants such as **ferns** grow from **spores (spawrz)**.  
- A spore is a single cell that can develop into a new plant exactly like the plant that produced it.  

### Seed Plants

- Produce seeds containing an **undeveloped plant** and stored food inside a protective coat.  
- Some seed plants produce **flowers**; others do not.

## Differences Between Seedless and Seed Plants

- **Mosses, ferns, and horsetails** are seedless plants that grow from spores found in spore cases on the undersides of fern leaves (fronds).  
- **Seed plants** produce **male and female cells**.  
- Seeds form when male and female cells join. Inside a seed is a new plant and food.  

---

## Classifying Seed Plants

- Seed plants that produce flowers are called **angiosperms (AN•jee•uh•spurmz)** (e.g., roses, orange trees).  
- Seed plants that do not produce flowers are called **gymnosperms (JIM•nuh•spurmz)**.  
- Gymnosperms produce seeds inside a **cone** (e.g., evergreens).

---

## What do flowers do?

- Flowers are used for **reproduction**—making new plants.  
- The flower is made of parts including the **stamen** (male part producing pollen grains) and the **pistil** (female part producing egg cells).  
- The **ovary** is the bottom part of the pistil where seeds form.

---

## Seeds: The Process

1. **Pollination:** Pollen grains move from the stamen to the pistil of the same or another flower.  
2. **Making a seed:** Pollen travels down into the ovary where pollen and egg cells join to form a seed. The ovary enlarges, becoming a fruit with seeds inside.  
3. **Scattering seeds:** Seeds are scattered by wind or animals. If conditions are right, seeds grow into new plants.

## Lesson 2: Plant Transport Systems

### How do materials move in plants?

- Inside vascular plants, tubes run through roots, stems, and leaves.  
- These tubes transport water, minerals, and food throughout the plant.

### Transport in Vascular Plants Diagram

- **Roots:** Take in water and minerals from soil.  
- **Stem (or Trunk):** Moves water and minerals **upward** from roots to leaves.  
- **Leaf:** Makes food (sugar).  
- **Stem (or Trunk):** Moves sugar **downward** to all parts of the plant.

---

## Roots

- Roots have tiny tubes made of vascular tissue.  
- **Xylem (ZIGH•luhm)** moves water and minerals up from the ground through the root to the stem.  
- Around the center, **phloem (FLO•em)** carries sugar from the leaves down the stem into the root.  
- Roots vary in size and shape:  
  - **Taproots:** thick roots that grow deep (e.g., carrots, beets).  
  - **Fibrous roots:** thin roots (e.g., grasses).  
  - **Prop roots:** finger-like roots (e.g., corn plants).

## 11. Why are two kinds of tubes needed in a plant?

Plants have **two kinds of tubes** to perform different essential functions. These tubes help in the **transport of substances** throughout the plant.

- One kind of tube is responsible for carrying **water and minerals** from the roots to other parts of the plant.
- The other kind transports **food produced in the leaves** to various parts of the plant for growth and storage.

These two types of tubes ensure the plant gets both the **nutrients** and **energy** it needs to survive.

### What is the transport system made of?  

You saw that a root has two kinds of tubes made from **vascular tissue**, **xylem** and **phloem**. They continue up from the root all the way through the stem.  

In different kinds of stems, the **xylem** and **phloem** are arranged differently. There is a layer of cells called **cambium** (KAM•be•uhm) in both stems. **Cambium** is where new cells of **xylem** and **phloem** are made.

- **Cambium** — makes new **xylem** and **phloem**  
- **Phloem** — brings materials down

### Tree Rings  

A tree stump gives you a view of the outside and inside of a **woody stem**, a tree trunk.  

- **Bark**: Along the outside is a layer of bark. Bark protects the trunk.  
- **Phloem**: Just inside the bark is a layer of phloem.  
- **Xylem**: Inside the phloem are rings. The rings are layers of xylem.  

A ring of **xylem** grows every year. By counting the rings you can tell the age of the tree. Start from the inside, the oldest part. Each ring has two parts:  
- **Light part**: Grows in the spring when water is usually plentiful.  
- **Dark part**: Grows in the fall when there is less rain.  

Counting tree rings gives you the age of a tree. The oldest living tree is a **bristlecone pine** in California. It is **4,767 years old**.

## Chapter 2 • Lesson 3  
### Photosynthesis and Respiration  
#### What do leaves do?  

A **leaf** is a factory that makes food (**sugar**). To make food, a leaf needs two raw materials: **water** and **carbon dioxide**.  

Look at the diagram to see how a leaf gets these two raw materials:  

- **Veins**: A plant takes in **water** from the soil. The water travels up the **xylem** through the roots and stem. The **xylem** goes into a leaf through narrow veins. **Water** enters the leaf through the **xylem**.  
- **Stomata** (STOH•muh•tuh): Tiny holes in the bottom of a leaf or stem. (One hole is called a **stoma**.) The stomata are surrounded by **guard cells**. When the guard cells open the stomata, **carbon dioxide** comes in. Guard cells can close the stomata to keep a plant from drying up.  

---

### Parts of a Leaf  

- **Vein**  
- **Epidermis**  
- **Guard cells**  
- **Stoma**  
- **Xylem**  
- **Phloem**  

---

### Food Making  

Here is an outline of the food-making process, **photosynthesis** (foh•tuh•SIN•thuh•sis):  

- **Where it happens**: Food is made in cells just under the **epidermis** (ep•i•DUR•mis), the outermost layer of a leaf. Food is made in **chloroplasts**, cell parts with a green substance that traps sunlight.  
- **What happens**: **Carbon dioxide** and **water** enter the chloroplasts. In the presence of sunlight, these two raw materials combine to form **sugar** and **oxygen**.  

  **carbon dioxide + water + energy → sugar + oxygen**  

- **After it happens**: **Phloem** carries the food to all parts of the plant. **Oxygen** goes out the stomata.

### What is a cycle in plants and animals?  

In **photosynthesis**, a plant makes food (**sugar**) and **oxygen**. These two products are used by the plant and also by animals.  

- **Sugar (food)**: The food has energy stored in it. Animals that eat plants take in the food with its stored energy. Other animals that eat plant-eaters also get the food and stored energy.  
- **Oxygen**: Plants and animals use oxygen for the process of **respiration** (res•puh•RAY•shuhn) in cells. **Respiration** is the release of energy from food.  

Respiration takes place in parts of a cell called **mitochondria**. **Oxygen** and **sugar** go into the mitochondria. The oxygen breaks down the sugar and energy is given off. Two waste products are made in the process: **carbon dioxide** and **water**.  

**sugar + oxygen → carbon dioxide + water + energy**

#### What is digestion?  

Every cell in your body needs energy to live and grow. This energy comes from **food**. However, food has to be broken down into a form your cells can use. Breaking down food into simpler substances that your body can use is called **digestion** (die•JES•chuhn).  

Here is how food reaches your cells:  

Your **digestive system** starts working on food in your mouth.  

1. When you bite into and chew food:  
   - Your **teeth** and **tongue** break the food into small pieces.  
   - Chemicals in your mouth break down some of the food into **nutrients**.  
   
   A **nutrient** is a simple form of food that your cells can use.  

2. The process continues in other organs where food is broken down further.  
3. The nutrients eventually pass into your blood. Blood carries them to your cells. The nutrients give your cells energy and materials for growing.

### What Does the Respiratory System Do?  

You breathe all the time, even while you sleep. Breathing is a job of your **respiratory system**. This system works to take in **oxygen** from the air and bring it to your **blood**. Your blood brings oxygen to all your **cells**. Here’s how it works:

- You have a muscle, the **diaphragm** (DIGH•uh•fram), that works to pull air in and push air out of your body.  
- When the diaphragm pulls down, you **inhale** (in•HAYL). Air enters your mouth and nose and fills your **lungs**, the main organs for breathing.  
- In the lungs, oxygen passes into the blood. The blood, in turn, drops off **carbon dioxide**, a waste gas, into your lungs.  
- When the diaphragm moves back up, you **exhale**. The carbon dioxide is pushed out of your body.

When you blow up a balloon, you are exhaling air with carbon dioxide.

## How do vessels and valves work?

## Chapter 4 • Lesson 2  
### The Water Cycle  
What makes water change form?  

You may think of water as only a liquid. However, water can be in three forms, or states:  
- **Solid (ice)**  
- **Liquid** (water in a glass or lake)  
- **Water vapor** (invisible gas in the air)  

Water can change states. Start with ice, such as ice on a pond in winter. When the pond heats up in spring and summer, the **ice melts**. It becomes liquid. Heat can also make liquid water in the pond change to water vapor.  

Heat is removed as the pond and the air above it get colder from summer to fall and winter. Water vapor in cooled air turns back into liquid and can fall as rain or snow. Changing a gas to a liquid is **condensation** (con•den•SAY•shuhn). As months get colder, more heat is removed from the pond. The pond freezes again.  

- Add heat: **melting**, **evaporation**  
- Remove heat: **freezing**, **condensation**

## Chapter 4 • Lesson 2  
### What happens to water after it evaporates?  

When water **evaporates**, it goes up into the air. If the air is moving, the moving air can carry it away. Moving air is **wind** or a **breeze**. What makes air move?  

Air is made up of particles of gases, such as oxygen. When heat is added to air, the particles move faster and spread apart. Cold air has **more particles** than the same amount of warm air.  

- Cold air is packed with more particles than warm air. Because cold air has more particles, cold air is **heavier** than an equal amount of warm air. So, **cold air sinks** while **warm air rises**.  

Large balloons can rise into the air because air inside of them is heated.  

---

At the shore during the day, sunlight warms land and water. Land warms up faster than water does.  
- As land heats up, air above it gets warmer. Warm air over land rises.  
- Air over water sinks. It moves toward land to replace the rising warm air. A **sea breeze** is felt moving from water to land.  

At night along the shore, land and water cool off. Land cools faster than water.  
- Air stays warmer longer over water. Warm air over water rises.  
- Air over land cools faster than air over water. Air sinks over land and moves toward water. A **land breeze** is felt moving from land toward water.

### Lesson 1: Earth's Atmosphere

#### What is Air Pressure?

The **atmosphere** is the layer of gases surrounding Earth, extending about **700 kilometers (435 miles)** from the surface. It contains five layers; the closest to Earth is the **troposphere**, where most **weather** occurs due to the crowding of gases.

**Air pressure** is the weight of air pressing on Earth's surface. At **sea level**, about **1 kilogram** of air presses down per square centimeter (almost 15 pounds per square inch). At higher altitudes such as mountaintops, air pressure decreases because there is less air above.

#### Factors Affecting Air Pressure

- **Amount of space:** Squeezing air into a smaller space increases air pressure.  
- **Height above Earth’s surface:** Air pressure decreases with altitude.  
- **Temperature:** Warm air expands and air pressure decreases; cold air contracts and air pressure increases.  
- **Humidity:** More **water vapor** (humidity) decreases air pressure, because water vapor weighs less than other gases.

#### Barometers

Barometers measure air pressure:  
- **Mercury barometers:** Air pressure pushes mercury up or down in a tube.  
- **Aneroid barometers:** Air pressure compresses an airtight container to indicate pressure changes.

---

### Lesson 2: Air Currents and Wind

#### Why Are Temperatures Different?

Because Earth is **spherical**, sunlight hits most directly at the **equator**, causing warmer temperatures near the equator compared to higher latitudes.

- **Direct sunlight** (90° angle) focuses energy on a smaller area, warming it more.  
- **Slanted sunlight** spreads over a wider area, producing less heat.

#### Sea and Land Breezes

- **Sea Breeze (Day):** Land heats up faster than water, so air pressure over land decreases; cooler air over water moves toward land.  
- **Land Breeze (Night):** Land cools faster, so air pressure over land increases; warmer air over water moves toward sea.

#### Global Winds

Global winds blow constantly in predictable directions due to pressure differences caused by temperature. One example:

- **Trade Winds:** Blow from **30°N (Tropic of Cancer)** toward the **equator**, moving air from higher to lower pressure zones.

---

### Lesson 3: Oceans and Air Temperature

Coastal areas experience milder climates because water heats and cools more slowly than land.

- In **summer**, coastal areas are cooler than inland.  
- In **winter**, coastal areas are warmer than inland.

#### Ocean Currents

Ocean currents move **warm** or **cold** water, affecting climate:

- **Warm currents** (e.g., Gulf Stream) warm nearby land.  
- **Cold currents** (e.g., California Current) cool nearby land.

#### El Niño

Typically, a cold current runs along Peru’s coast, maintaining high pressure and cool air. Every 2–7 years, during **El Niño**, warm surface water replaces the cold current, reversing wind patterns and bringing storms to the Americas.

---

### Lesson 4: Severe Weather

#### What Causes Severe Weather?

Severe weather is caused by the interaction of different **air masses**—large bodies of air with consistent temperature and humidity.

---

#### Fronts

- **Warm Front:** Warm air moves over colder air, forming layers of clouds and light rain or snow.  
- **Cold Front:** Cold air pushes under warm air, causing towering clouds and storms.

#### Thunderstorms

- Form when warm, humid air rises, cools, and condenses into **thunderheads**.  
- Lightning forms from static electricity caused by friction between updrafts and downdrafts inside clouds.  
- Thunder is the sound of rapidly expanding heated air.

#### Tornadoes

- Form when warm air rises in a thunderhead, creating low pressure that pulls in spinning air, forming a **funnel cloud**.  
- If the funnel touches the ground, it becomes a **tornado**, with winds up to 480 km/h (299 mph).  
- Most common in **Tornado Alley**, where cold air from Canada meets warm, moist air from the Gulf of Mexico.  

**Related Terms:**  
- **Funnel cloud:** spinning cloud not touching ground  
- **Tornado:** funnel cloud touching the ground  
- **Cyclone:** storm with low pressure and spinning winds  
- **Thunderhead:** towering cloud that brings storms

#### Hurricanes

- Begin as **tropical storms** over warm ocean waters.  
- When winds exceed 117 km/h (73 mph), they become **hurricanes**.  
- Characterized by a spiral of clouds with a calm **eye** at the center.  
- Causes a **storm surge**, a rise in ocean height that can flood coasts.

## Lesson 5: Predicting the Weather

### Who needs to know what the weather will be?

You need to know what the **weather** will be so you can plan your day. So do people who work outdoors—**builders**, **truck drivers**, and **letter carriers**. **Airplane pilots** need to know the weather to make safe takeoffs and landings. **Farmers** must know the weather months ahead of time to plan crops to plant.

### How can scientists forecast (FOR•kast) tomorrow’s weather?

To **forecast** means to “make a guess about what will happen based on what you know and see.” For example, if you see something happen day after day, you may **forecast** that it will happen tomorrow.

### Lesson 1: The Sun

#### What is the Sun?

### Gravity and Orbit  

#### What is Gravity?  
Why do you fall when you trip? You fall because of the **pull of gravity** between you and **Earth**. **Gravity** is a pull between any two objects.  

There is gravity throughout the **solar system**. For example, there is a pull of gravity between the **Sun** and each **planet**. The strength of gravity depends on:  
- **Distance**: The closer two objects are to each other, the greater the pull is. The pull gets weaker when objects are farther apart.  
- **Mass**: Mass means “how much matter” is in something. The greater the total mass of any two objects, the stronger the pull of gravity is between the two objects.  

Suppose you traveled from Earth to the Moon. Where is gravity stronger: on **Earth** or on the **Moon**?  
The astronaut, **John Young**, could jump higher on the Moon than on Earth. Why?  
The pull of gravity is less on the Moon.  

---

### Gravity and Weight  
Measuring your **weight** on a scale can help you compare gravity on Earth and the Moon. Your weight depends on two things:  
- Your **mass** (in kilograms)  
- The pull of **gravity** where you are  

If you go to the Moon, your **mass** stays the same. However, Earth has more **mass** than the Moon. So the total mass of you and Earth is more than the total mass of you and the Moon. So gravity is stronger on Earth. You weigh more on Earth than on the Moon.  

The huge planet **Jupiter** has more mass than Earth. The pull of gravity on Jupiter is greater than on Earth. If you visited Jupiter, your mass would stay the same; however, you would weigh much more than on Earth.  

- The boy’s **mass** is always the same.  
- His **weight** more than doubles on Jupiter.  
- His weight on the Moon is only about **1/6** his weight on Earth.

### What Keeps Objects in Orbit?  
**Planets** travel around the Sun in almost circular paths. **Moons** travel around planets in similar kinds of paths. The path one object takes around another is called an **orbit**.  

Objects are held in their orbits by **gravity**. For example, planets are held in their orbits around the Sun by the pull of gravity between each planet and the Sun.  

The pull of gravity alone would pull a planet into the Sun. It takes **gravity** and **inertia** (in•UR•shuh) together to keep objects in their orbits.  

- **Inertia** is a way in which objects act when they move or stay at rest.  
- A moving object tends to keep moving in a straight line.  
- An object at rest tends to stay at rest.  

---

#### Gravity and Inertia  

- Gravity alone would pull Earth into the Sun.  
- Inertia alone would make Earth move forward in a straight line.  
- Gravity and inertia together make Earth follow this path.  

---

### Working Together  
How do **gravity** and **inertia** work together? Think of a space vehicle orbiting Earth. **Gravity** is pulling the vehicle toward Earth. However, the vehicle and the crew don’t feel this pull. The crew members are **weightless**.  

Gravity is being balanced by the forward motion of the vehicle. In the same way, as planets orbit the Sun, gravity would pull them toward the Sun. However, the forward motion of the planets keeps them moving away from the Sun.  

These two motions make planets move in nearly circular orbits. The shape of the orbit is an **ellipse**, a flattened circle. Because the orbit is not a perfect circle, Earth is farther from the Sun at certain times of the year than at other times.

### What Causes Tides?  
You have learned that there is a pull of gravity between any two objects—such as between **Earth** and the **Sun**. However, there is also a pull of gravity between Earth and the **Moon**. Both of these pulls have an effect on Earth.  

The Moon has much less mass than the Sun, but it is much closer to Earth. The pull between Earth and the Moon is about **twice as strong** as the pull between Earth and the Sun.  

The pull is felt on Earth’s oceans. This pull causes **tides**. A **tide** is a rise and fall of the ocean’s surface. Most oceans have two high tides and two low tides each 24-hour day.  

Earth spins on its axis all the time, making a complete spin in one day. As any point spins to face the Moon, ocean water bulges on that side and the opposite side (high tides). In between the bulges are the low tides.  

- **High tide**  
- **Low tide**

## Chapter 7 • Lesson 3  
### Classifying Elements

#### What Are Atoms Made of?

- **Protons (PROH•tahns)** and **neutrons (NEW•trons)** are located in the **nucleus** of an atom. Protons carry a **positive** electrical charge; neutrons are uncharged.
- **Electrons (e•LEK•trahns)** move around the nucleus quickly and carry a **negative charge**.

The **atomic number** is the number of protons in an atom. Different elements have different atomic numbers.

- Example:  
  - Helium has 2 protons.  
  - Carbon has 6 protons.  
  - Oxygen has 8 protons, 8 electrons, and 8 neutrons (atomic weight = 16).  
  - Boron has 4 protons, 4 electrons, and 3 neutrons (atomic weight = 8).

Each element has atoms with its own number of protons, which equals the number of electrons.

---

### Atomic Weight and Molecules

- The **atomic weight** is the sum of protons and neutrons.
- Electrons are not counted because they have very little mass.
- Some elements naturally exist as **molecules**—particles made of more than one atom joined together.
  - For example, an oxygen molecule (**O₂**) is made of 2 oxygen atoms.
  - Water molecules (**H₂O**) are made of 2 hydrogen atoms and 1 oxygen atom.

---

### What Are Properties of Elements?

- Most elements are **metals**: conduct heat and electricity well, can be bent or flattened, usually solids at room temperature (e.g., aluminum, gold, iron, copper, silver).
- There are 17 **nonmetals**: poor conductors, solids like carbon break rather than bend, many are gases (helium, oxygen, nitrogen), bromine is a liquid.
- **Metalloids** conduct heat and electricity but not as well as metals (e.g., boron, silicon).

---

### Can We See Atoms?

- Atoms are incredibly small; a pinch of salt contains over half a billion sodium and chlorine atoms.
- Special microscopes allow us to see atoms:
  - **Electron microscope** (invented 1932) uses electrons to see molecules.
  - **Field ion microscope** (invented 1951) uses ions to visualize molecules and large atoms.
  - **Scanning tunneling microscope** uses a fine tip to move atoms individually.
  - **One-angstrom microscope** shows atoms lined up inside metals.

---

### What is the Periodic Table?

- **Dmitri Mendeleev** created the periodic table in the 1800s, organizing elements by increasing atomic mass.
- Elements in columns (groups) have similar properties.
- The periodic table arranges elements by increasing **atomic number**.
- Properties repeat periodically as you move across rows.

---

### Similar Elements in the Periodic Table

- Metals are grouped (blue boxes).
- Nonmetals are grouped (green boxes).
- Metalloids are grouped (yellow boxes).
- Gases are grouped (symbols in red).
- Groups or families have similar properties:
  - **Group 17**: Halogen gases; foul-smelling, burn flesh, combine with metals.
  - **Group 18**: Noble gases; inert, do not easily combine with other elements.

## Chapter 7 • Lesson 4  
### Mixtures

#### What is a Mixture?

- A **mixture** is a combination of two or more things that keep their own properties.
- Examples:  
  - **Trail mix** (nuts and pretzels) can be separated easily.  
  - **Tossed salad** varies in composition throughout.  
  - **Milk** has particles too small to see.  
  - **Concrete** is a solid mixture of rocks, sand, cement powder, and water.

---

### To Settle or Not to Settle

- A **suspension** is a mixture where particles settle out over time (e.g., oil and vinegar mixture).
- A **solution** is a mixture with particles as small as atoms or molecules that do not settle (e.g., sugar dissolved in water, window cleaner).

---

### What Are the Parts of a Solution?

- The **solvent (SOL•vuhnt)** is the part that dissolves another substance (usually the part in the largest amount), like water.
- The **solute (SOL•yewt)** is the part that gets dissolved, like sugar.
- Both solutes and solvents can be solids, liquids, or gases.
- Example: In air, nitrogen is the solvent and oxygen is the solute.

---

### Reaching a Limit

- Solvents can dissolve only a **certain amount** of solute at a given temperature.
- Example: At room temperature, water dissolves only 37 grams of salt per 100 grams of water.
- Heating water can increase the amount of **solid solute** that dissolves.
- However, heating decreases the amount of **gas solute** (e.g., warm seltzer holds less carbon dioxide than cold seltzer).

## What are acids and bases?

- An orange tastes sour. Squeeze a drop of orange juice on **litmus (LIT•muhs) paper**. Lemon juice makes the paper turn **red**.

- **Litmus paper** is an **indicator** (IN•duh•kay•duhr). An indicator changes color in ways to help you tell what a substance is.

- The red color indicates that orange juice is an **acid**.

- An **acid** is a substance that tastes sour and turns litmus paper **red**. Be careful: Never taste unfamiliar substances to tell if they are acids.

- Other acids are lemon juice and vinegar.

- The formula for any acid starts with **H (hydrogen)**. For example, **hydrochloric acid** is **HCl**.

- When you mix an acid and water, **hydrogen particles** are formed. The hydrogen particles have an electric charge. They conduct electricity through water.

  `acid + water → H⁺`

- Citrus fruits (such as oranges and lemons) contain an **acid**.

---

## Bases

- Soap and ammonia cleaner contain **bases**.

- A **base** is a substance that tastes bitter and turns litmus paper **blue**.

- Bases feel slippery, like soap. Be careful: Never taste or feel unfamiliar substances to tell if they are bases.

- The formula for a base ends in **OH** (oxygen + hydrogen).

- When a base is added to water, a charged particle is formed from the OH.

- These charged particles carry electricity in water.

  `base + water → OH⁻`

- When an acid is mixed with a base, they form a **salt**.

- The acid supplies the **nonmetal** part of the salt. The base supplies the **metal** part.

- Acids and bases **neutralize** (NEW•truh•lyze) each other.  
  **Neutralize** means “to cancel each other out.” That is, the salt that is produced is not an acid or a base.

## Chapter 8 • Lesson 3  
### How strong are acids and bases?

Some **acids** are stronger than others. For example, a **strong acid** can wear away a hole in metal quickly. **Vinegar**, on the other hand, is a **weak acid**. It’s weak enough for you to use on a salad with no effect.

Some **bases** are stronger than others. For example, **lye** is a **strong base** in drain cleaners. It can “eat away” a clog in a drain quickly.

The strength of **acids** and **bases** is measured on a **pH scale**. The scale runs from **0** (strong acid, weak base) to **14** (strong base, weak acid). A rating of **7**, right in the middle, is **neutral**—neither acid nor base.

### pH Scale of Charged Hydrogen Particles

- At low pH (**acids**), there are more **H⁺** than **OH⁻** particles.  
- At high pH (**bases**), there are more **OH⁻** than **H⁺**.  
- At **7**, there are equal numbers of both.

## Changes in Matter  
### Reading the pH Scale

**Strong acids** form many charged **hydrogen particles (H⁺)** when added to water. They have very few **OH⁻** particles. For example, an acid with a pH of **0** or **1** forms many more **H⁺** than an acid with a pH of **5** or **6**.

**Strong bases** have very few **H⁺** particles in water. They form, instead, many **OH⁻** particles. Bases with a pH of **13** or **14** have many more **OH⁻** particles than a base with a pH of **8** or **9**.

**Water** has a pH of **7**. It has about the same number of **H⁺** and **OH⁻** particles. Water is **neutral**. That is, it is neither an acid nor a base.

Scientists use **meters** to measure the pH of water and soil. A pH near **0** (very acid) can be very harmful for living things in a lake or river. Most plants grow best when the soil has a pH **over 7 (base)** rather than under (acid).

## Chapter 8 • Lesson 3  
### How do we use salts?

**Salt** was used as **money** in some ancient cultures. Why was it so valuable? In days when there were no freezers, salt kept foods from **spoiling**. Salts remove water from foods. **Bacteria** cannot survive in foods dried with salt. **Fish** has been packed in salt in many places for centuries.

Salt is used for **seasoning**. Small amounts of salt along with other flavorings give many meals a **rich flavor**. Salt is also used for **curing meats** and **baking**. It is used for **canning foods** and **pickling foods**.

Salt is also very useful in **icy weather**. If you spread salt onto ice, it dissolves into the ice and **lowers the freezing point**. The ice turns to slush or water and is easy to remove.

**Mummies** were dried in salt by ancient people of **Egypt**. A number of them have remained preserved for over **2,000 years**.

Salt spreaders are hard at work to ease the removal of snow and ice from the road.

### Getting Salt

Combining an **acid** and a **base** makes a **salt**. However, to meet our need for salt, we collect salt that is already made in nature.

Salt was formed early in **Earth’s history**. It was dissolved by rain and ended up in the **oceans**. Today, there are as much as **3.5 kg (7.7 lb) of salt** in every **100 kg (220 lb) of ocean water**.

In many places today, ocean water is drawn into **shallow pools**. Exposed to the **Sun**, the water **evaporates**. The salt remains behind.

Early in Earth’s history, salt remained behind when shallow inland seas dried up. Over time the salt was buried by **sediments**.

We can get this salt by pumping water down into the salt. The water becomes salty. We collect the water and let it **evaporate**. The salt remains behind.
